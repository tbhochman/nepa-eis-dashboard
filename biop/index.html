ESA Section 7 Has a Data Problem

Data Quality: Evidence of Unreliable Public Reporting

The TAILS Gap what USFWS logs internally vs. what it publishes

The Tracking and Integrated Logging System (TAILS) is USFWS's internal consultation database. Since approximately 2007, all FWS field offices have been required to log every Section 7 consultation — formal and informal — in TAILS. The system is used by roughly 1,400 FWS biologists across all regional and field offices. Per the only comprehensive academic audit (Malcom & Li, 2015, PNAS), TAILS logged 88,290 consultations (6,829 formal) from January 2008 through April 2015 — an average of ~935 formal biological opinions per year.

ECOSPHERE, the public-facing system, contains a species-linked subset: only those opinions that field staff manually connect to a listed species record. This is a separate, voluntary data entry step on top of the mandatory TAILS logging. The result is a massive and inconsistent gap between what USFWS produces and what it publishes.

If ECOSPHERE reflected actual consultation volumes, we'd expect relatively consistent reporting from each office. Instead, the data shows offices that report heavily for a few years, then stop entirely — and others that appear for the first time a decade into mandatory TAILS reporting. This pattern is diagnostic of voluntary, inconsistent data entry, not real changes in ESA workload.

Key examples:
South Florida: 72 entries in 2018 → 0 from 2023 onward. Did South Florida stop having endangered species consultations? No — staff stopped entering data in ECOSPHERE.
Sacramento: 130 entries in 2018 (the single largest contributor to the "peak year") → 41–50 in recent years, a 60% drop.
Maine: Zero entries before 2023 → 41 in 2023. Maine didn't suddenly start having ESA consultations — someone started entering data.
West Virginia: Zero entries before 2024 → 43 in 2025. Same pattern.
Ohio: 54 in 2018 → single digits by 2022. No biological explanation for a 90% decline in formal consultations.

What This Means implications for ESA transparency

TAILS is mandatory; ECOSPHERE is not. Every FWS biologist must log consultations in TAILS — but the additional step of linking those opinions to species records in the public-facing ECOSPHERE system is discretionary. There is no enforcement mechanism, no completeness audit, and no consequence for not entering data. The result: USFWS produces nearly 1,000 formal biological opinions per year, but the public can only see roughly a third of them.

The prior public system (ECOS) was even worse. ECOS froze entirely in January 2022 and received no new data for over a year. ECOSPHERE, its successor, began a phased migration in 2020 that is still not complete. During the transition, 16 ECOS records (all multi-species opinions from South Florida) were lost entirely — they exist in neither system.

The gold standard remains locked inside USFWS. The only comprehensive analysis of TAILS data was published by Malcom & Li in 2015, through a data-sharing agreement with the agency. They found that only 2 of 6,829 formal consultations (0.03%) resulted in jeopardy findings — a statistic impossible to verify or update from public data because ECOSPHERE does not include jeopardy/no-jeopardy outcomes.

This is a transparency problem. The Endangered Species Act's consultation process affects billions of dollars in federal actions annually. The public reporting system captures a fraction of those consultations, with no consistency guarantees and no outcomes data. Any analysis of ESA consultation trends based on ECOS or ECOSPHERE data — including this dashboard — should be understood as reflecting data entry patterns, not the actual operation of the Endangered Species Act.
